# Classification of Groups of Order 4p

## Overview

This document presents the complete classification of finite groups of order $4p$, where $p$ is an odd prime. The classification was established using the **Cayley table method** (莱凯表法) combined with Sylow theory and number-theoretic analysis of congruence equations.

The key results are:
- When $p \equiv 1 \pmod{4}$: there exist **5 distinct types** of groups of order $4p$
- When $p \equiv 3 \pmod{4}$ and $p \neq 3$: there exist **4 distinct types** of groups of order $4p$
- When $|G| = 12$ (i.e., $p = 3$): $G$ is either isomorphic to the alternating group $A_4$, or the Sylow-3 subgroup is normal, yielding **4 additional structures**

---

## Preliminary Analysis

Let $G$ be a finite group with $|G| = 4p$, where $p$ is an odd prime. Let $S_p \in \text{Syl}_p(G)$ denote a Sylow-$p$ subgroup and $A \in \text{Syl}_2(G)$ denote a Sylow-2 subgroup.

### Sylow Subgroup Analysis

By **Sylow's Third Theorem**:
- $n_2 = 2k + 1 \mid 4p \Rightarrow n_2 \in \{1, p\}$
- $n_p = pk + 1 \mid 4p \Rightarrow n_p = 1$

Since $n_p = 1$, the Sylow-$p$ subgroup $S_p$ is **normal in $G$**. As $|S_p| = p$ (prime), $S_p$ is cyclic: $S_p = \langle g \rangle \cong C_p$ where $g^p = 1$.

Thus $G$ is an extension of the cyclic group $S_p$ by a group $A$ of order 4. The Sylow-2 subgroup $A$ can be either:
1. **Cyclic**: $A = \langle a \rangle \cong C_4$ with $a^4 = 1$
2. **Klein four-group**: $A = \langle a \rangle \times \langle b \rangle \cong K_4$ with $a^2 = b^2 = 1$, $[a,b] = e$

### The Action of $A$ on $S_p$

Since $S_p \trianglelefteq G$, conjugation by elements of $A$ induces an action on $S_p$. For any $a \in A$ and $g \in S_p$:

$$a^{-1}ga = g^m$$

for some integer $m$. This defines an inner automorphism $\phi \in \text{Inn}(G)$.

**Key Congruence Equation**: When $A = \langle a \rangle \cong C_4$ is cyclic:

$$a^{-4}ga^4 = g^{m^4} = g \Rightarrow m^4 \equiv 1 \pmod{p}$$

Factoring: $m^4 - 1 = (m^2 - 1)(m^2 + 1)$, yielding two congruence equations:
- $m^2 \equiv 1 \pmod{p}$
- $m^2 \equiv -1 \pmod{p}$

The solvability depends on $p \pmod{4}$:

| Condition | $m^2 \equiv 1 \pmod{p}$ | $m^2 \equiv -1 \pmod{p}$ |
|-----------|------------------------|--------------------------|
| $p \equiv 1 \pmod{4}$ | Solutions: $m = \pm 1$ | Two additional solutions exist |
| $p \equiv 3 \pmod{4}$, $p \neq 3$ | Solutions: $m = \pm 1$ | **No solutions** |

---

## Main Classification Theorem

> **Theorem**: Let $G$ be a finite group with $|G| = 4p$, where $p$ is an odd prime.
>
> **(a)** If $p \equiv 1 \pmod{4}$, then $G$ has exactly **5 non-isomorphic types**.
>
> **(b)** If $p \equiv 3 \pmod{4}$ and $p \neq 3$, then $G$ has exactly **4 non-isomorphic types**.
>
> **(c)** If $|G| = 12$ ($p = 3$), then $G$ is either isomorphic to $A_4$ or the Sylow-3 subgroup is normal, yielding 4 structures from cases (a)-(b).

---

## The Five Types of Groups of Order 4p

### Type I: Cyclic Group $C_{4p}$

$$G = \langle a \rangle, \quad a^{4p} = 1$$

- **Abelian**, Sylow-2 subgroup $A = \langle a^p \rangle \cong C_4$ (cyclic)
- $G \cong C_4 \times C_p \cong C_{4p}$ (since $\gcd(4,p) = 1$)
- The group is generated by a single element of order $4p$
- **Exists for all odd primes $p$**

---

### Type II: Elementary Abelian-Type Group $C_2 \times C_{2p}$

$$G = \langle a \rangle \times \langle g \rangle, \quad a^2 = g^{2p} = 1$$

or equivalently (when $p > 2$):

$$G = \langle a \rangle \times \langle b \rangle \times \langle h \rangle \cong C_2 \times C_2 \times C_p$$

where $h = g^2$ has order $p$.

- **Abelian**, Sylow-2 subgroup $A \cong C_2 \times C_2 \cong K_4$ (Klein four-group)
- All non-identity elements have order 2 or $p$
- $G$ is the direct product of its Sylow subgroups: $G = S_p \times A$
- **Exists for all odd primes $p$**

---

### Type III: Dihedral-Type Group (Non-Abelian, $A \cong C_4$)

$$G = \langle a, g \mid a^4 = g^p = 1, \; a^{-1}ga = g^{-1} \rangle$$

- **Non-abelian**, Sylow-2 subgroup $A = \langle a \rangle \cong C_4$ (cyclic)
- The conjugation action is $a^{-1}ga = g^{-1}$ (corresponding to $m = -1$)
- When $p = 2$, this reduces to the dihedral group $D_8$; for general odd $p$, it is a **generalized dicyclic-type** construction
- The relation $a^{-1}ga = g^{-1}$ captures the "reflection" action on the cyclic normal subgroup
- **Exists for all odd primes $p$**

**Alternative presentation**: This group can be viewed as a semidirect product $C_p \rtimes C_4$ where the generator of $C_4$ acts by inversion on $C_p$.

---

### Type IV: Special Non-Abelian Group (Only when $p \equiv 1 \pmod{4}$)

$$G = \langle a, g \mid a^4 = g^p = 1, \; a^{-1}ga = g^l \rangle$$

where $l^2 \equiv -1 \pmod{p}$ (i.e., $l$ is a square root of $-1$ modulo $p$).

- **Non-abelian**, Sylow-2 subgroup $A = \langle a \rangle \cong C_4$ (cyclic)
- The conjugation action is $a^{-1}ga = g^l$ where $l \not\equiv \pm 1 \pmod{p}$ and $l^2 \equiv -1 \pmod{p}$
- **Only exists when $p \equiv 1 \pmod{4}$**, since $-1$ is a quadratic residue mod $p$ if and only if $p \equiv 1 \pmod{4}$ (by Euler's criterion / quadratic reciprocity)
- When two solutions $l$ and $l' = p - l$ exist, they yield **isomorphic groups** (since $l' \equiv -l \pmod{p}$)
- This is the group structure that distinguishes the $p \equiv 1 \pmod{4}$ case from the $p \equiv 3 \pmod{4}$ case

---

### Type V: Non-Abelian Group with $A \cong K_4$

$$G = \langle a, b, g \mid a^2 = b^2 = g^p = 1, \; [a,b] = [a,g] = e, \; b^{-1}gb = g^{-1} \rangle$$

Equivalently: $G = \langle a \rangle \times \langle g, b \rangle$ where $a^2 = 1$ and $\langle g, b \rangle$ is a dihedral-type group of order $2p$.

- **Non-abelian**, Sylow-2 subgroup $A = \langle a \rangle \times \langle b \rangle \cong K_4$ (Klein four-group)
- The element $a$ commutes with everything (central involution), while $b$ acts by inversion on $S_p$
- $G \cong C_2 \times D_{2p}$ where $D_{2p}$ is the dihedral group of order $2p$
- **Exists for all odd primes $p$**

**Verification of uniqueness**: When $A \cong K_4 = \langle a, b \rangle$, we must determine $a^{-1}ga = g^r$ and $b^{-1}gb = g^l$ with $r^2 \equiv l^2 \equiv 1 \pmod{p}$, so $r, l \in \{\pm 1\}$. The cases are:

| $(r, l)$ | Structure |
|----------|-----------|
| $(1, 1)$ | Abelian (Type II) |
| $(-1, 1)$ or $(1, -1)$ | **Type V** (isomorphic cases) |
| $(-1, -1)$ | Same as Type V (by changing basis of $K_4$) |

The key observation is that replacing $b$ with $ab$ transforms the $(-1, -1)$ case into the $(-1, 1)$ case, proving they yield the same group structure.

## The Special Case $|G| = 12$ ($p = 3$)

When $p = 3$, we have $|G| = 12 = 2^2 \cdot 3$. In addition to the 4 types from the general classification (since $3 \equiv 3 \pmod{4}$, Type IV does not exist), there is one exceptional case:

### The Alternating Group $A_4$

$$G \cong A_4$$

**Lemma**: A group of order 12 is either isomorphic to $A_4$ or has a normal Sylow-3 subgroup (the latter case corresponds to Types I-III and V above).

**Proof sketch**: If $n_3 \neq 1$, then by Sylow's theorems, $n_3 = 4$. This means $G$ has four Sylow-3 subgroups $P_1, P_2, P_3, P_4$, pairwise intersecting trivially. The conjugation action of $G$ on $\{P_1, P_2, P_3, P_4\}$ induces a homomorphism $\phi: G \to S_4$ with trivial kernel, and $\phi(G) \cap A_4$ contains at least 9 elements, forcing $G \cong A_4$.

**Properties of $A_4$**:
- $|A_4| = 12$
- $n_2 = 1$: the Sylow-2 subgroup is normal and $\cong K_4$
- $n_3 = 4$: the Sylow-3 subgroups are **not** normal
- $A_4$ has no subgroup of order 6
- $A_4' = K_4$ (the commutator subgroup is the Klein four-group)

## Number-Theoretic Foundations

The classification relies on three fundamental theorems from number theory (from Vinogradov's *Elements of Number Theory*):

**Theorem 1**: $p \equiv 1 \pmod{4}$ if and only if there exists $l \in \{1, 2, \ldots, \frac{p-1}{2}\}$ such that $l^2 \equiv -1 \pmod{p}$. The two solutions are $l$ and $l' = p - l$.

**Theorem 2**: $p \equiv 3 \pmod{4}$ and $p \neq 3$ if and only if $l^2 \equiv -1 \pmod{p}$ has **no solutions**.

**Theorem 3**: For any odd prime $p$, the congruence $l^2 \equiv 1 \pmod{p}$ has exactly two solutions: $l = \pm 1$.

## Methodological Notes

The **Cayley table method** (莱凯表法) provides an elementary approach to this classification:

1. Establish that $S_p = \langle g \rangle \trianglelefteq G$ (Sylow-$p$ is normal)
2. Enumerate elements using a basis: $e, a, b, g, g^2, \ldots, g^{p-1}, ag, ag^2, \ldots$
3. Determine the conjugation action $a^{-1}ga = g^m$ by analyzing the constraint $m^4 \equiv 1 \pmod{p}$
4. Case-split based on the structure of $A$ (cyclic $C_4$ vs. Klein $K_4$)
5. Solve the resulting congruence equations to enumerate all possible group structures

This method avoids the complexity of group extension theory and automorphism group computations, making the classification accessible with only Sylow theory and basic number theory.
